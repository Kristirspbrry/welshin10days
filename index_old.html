4th DAY - Greetings

Let's learn about hellos and introductions, it's only polite! One thing to note is that some words and phrases may appear slightly different depending on where you are in Wales.

For example we say 'sut mae' for hello in south Wales, but in north Wales its 'su'mae'. However, there are actually numerous ways you can say hello - 'helô' and 'haia' are also widely spoken.

A more informal, shorter version of 'sut mae' is 's'mae'. Often you may just hear 'Wyt ti’n iawn?' which is an informal way to ask your friends or family how they are doing.

Sut mae

Hello

Sut wyt ti?

How are you?

Gwych, diolch

Great, thanks

'Hwal' literally means 'fun', which is how we say goodbye in Wales. You may want to add 'i ty' which means 'to you', and is often used between friends. A more formal version, when speaking to someone you may not know so well would be 'hwyl i chi'.

Hwyl i ty

Bye

Every greeting in Welsh begins with 'da' or 'dda' which means 'good'. 'Da' is the masculine form and 'dda' is the feminine form.

As we have learned before in the previous lesson, by adding 'nos' we transition into night.

Bore da i ty

Good morning to you

Prynhawn da i chi

Good afternoon to you

Noswaith dda

Good evening

Nos da

Good night

When introducing yourself, in Welsh we say the name first. The sentence structures are a little bit different in Welsh, but we will learn more about that later too. And just a small note, 'creoso' can mean both - 'welcome' and 'you're welcome'.

Croeso!

Welcome!

Beth ydy eich enw chi?

What is your name?

Draig dw i, a chi?

I'm Dragon, and you?

Jac dw i, dw i'n flewog

I am Jack, I am hairy

And that's it for this lesson! We will look into sentence structure and word mutations a lot more in the coming lessons.

5th DAY - FOOD AND EATING

Welcome to the fifth lesson! This chapter will be all about expressing what you like and want to eat. Let's begin with some basics and then we can move into building sentences and asking questions.

Bwyta

Yfed

Hoff fwyd

Hoff ddiod

Favourite drink

Now let's see how we can introduce those words into sentences. To say 'like' in Welsh is 'hoffi'.

Dw i'n hoffi yfed dŵr

I like to drink water

Dw i'n hoffi mefus

I like strawberries

Dw i'n bwyta cinio

I am eating lunch

Let's look at how to say what you may like and may not like. Saying 'please' in Welsh may appear like a bit of a mouthful - 'os gwelwycg yn dda', but with some practice it'll get easy! Its literal translation is - 'if seen as good', and you will find quite a few interesting expressions like that in Welsh.

Y cawl, os gwelwycg

The soup, please.

Dych ch'n hoffi bwyta caws?

Do you like to eat cheese?

Dim diolch, dw i fegan

No thanks, I am a vegan.

The expression for 'no, I do not' in Welsh is 'nac ydw'. 'Ydw' means 'yes' but it also stands for 'I am'. In English we say 'no' or 'yes', however in Welsh we say 'I am' or 'I am not'.

Nac ydw, dw i ddim eisiau reis

No, I don't want rice

Dw i ddim yn orenau

I don't enjoy oranges

Ydw, dw i eisiau pasta, diolch

Yes, I want pasta, thank you

To say 'or' in Welsh it's 'neu'. And to say 'and' it's simply just 'a'. 'Dych chi' stands for 'are you' or 'do you', depending on context.

Te a coffi

Tea and coffee

Dych chi eisiau lemon neu oren?

Do you want lemon or orange?

Sudd ffrwythau, os gwelwycg

Fruit juice, please

You will notice a little caret accent on the letters 'ŵ' and 'ŷ', like in the word below. In Welsh, we call this 'to bach' and it means that we should pronounce that sound a little longer.

Dŵr

Water

And that's all for this lesson! What are your favourite foods to eat? See you if can find how to say them in Welsh and combine them into the sentences you have already learned.

6th DAY - Out and about

Croeso! You are on to lesson 6. In this lesson we will look at how to say where you are going, as well as the all important phrases you may need while out and about. We will be picking up the pace now and learning full sentences. Do go over each one carefully and see if you can pick up on some new words too.

Dw i yma

I am here

Dw i'n dod o..

I come from..

You may notice that for the above, one of the expressions starts with 'Dw i' and the other with 'Dw i'n'. We use 'i'n' when we are in the process of doing something (before an adjective). But we we 'i' for anything that's to do with a noun or when we are describing ourselves.

In Welsh, we have several ways of saying 'I am going to..' when referring to a place. Whenever we are talking about going to a place, like a town, we use 'i'.

Dw i'n mynd i London

I am going to London

But if we are going to visit a person for example, we use the word 'at', just like the example below.

Dw i'n mynd at Mam-gu

I am going to my Gran's

Another thing to note is that some town and city names may be different in Welsh. We will have a look at a few right now, but is there a town you would really want to visit in Wales? What is it called?

Yng Nghaerdydd

in Cardiff

Yn Abertawe

In Swansea

Now, let's mend together all the different parts we have learned to form longer sentences.

Dych ch'in mynd i Nghaerdydd yfory?

Are you going to Cardiff tonight?

Another thing to note is that we also have different ways of saying 'in'. We use the word 'mewn' when describing something non specific. In english it would be 'in a'. But we use the word 'yn / y' when describing something more specific, like 'in the..'

Ble dach ch'in byw?

Where do you live?

Dw i'n byw yn y pentre

I live in the village

Yma, mewn Cymru

Here, in Wales

You will also notice below that we say 'mae'r' in one of the sentences and 'maer'n' in another. 'Mae'n' here simply means 'it is'. However, the ''r' in 'mae'r' stands for 'the'. You may remember that we use y / yn / 'r to say 'the'. Which one we choose depends on wovels or consonants in the words surrounding it. So in this instance we must use ''r', because the word before it ends with a vowel. If, the word before 'the' ends with a consonant and the one after it begins with a consonant we use 'y' (for exmaple: Dw i’n gweld y car - I see the car). However, if the word before 'the' ends with a consonant but the one after it begins with a vowel, we use 'yr' (for example: Dw i'n gweld yr wyddor - I see the alphabet.)

Dw i'n gweld by car

I see a car

Dw i'n aros am yr wyddor

I see the alphabet

In Welsh, there are seven wovels (remember 'w' is a vowel because of how it is pronounced): a, e, i, o, u, w, and y.

Now let's look at some differences in expression you may see in north Wales and south Wales. For example, the word for 'where'. In South Wales, we use 'ble' and in North Wales we use 'lle'.

Y siop newydd

The new shop

Lle mae'r siop fara?

Where is the bakery?

Pryd mae'n cau?

When does it close?

There are some words in Welsh that may have several meanings. Let's look at the word 'aros'. It can mean 'to wait', however, it also stands for 'stay', depending on the context.

Nac ydw, Dw i'n mynd i aros yma

No, I am going to stay here

Dw i'n aros am y bws

I am waiting for the bus

And that's it! Hopefully you've learned some useful words and phrases that you can build more upon. Do you have a preference on learning more north Wales or south Wales expressions? Does one seem harder than the other?

So, for this lesson, we will dedicate our full attention to learning about the time and see what sort of sentences we can come up with too.

Y nos

In Welsh, time is of a feminine origin, so when we use the expression 'mae hi'n'. 'Hi'n' means 'she' and 'mae hi'n' stands for 'it is' in this instance.

Faint o'r gloch ydy hi?

What time is it?

Mae hi'n deuddeg o'r gloch

It is twelve o-clock

Mae hi'n amser gwly

It is bedtime

In Welsh, we say 'weudi' when the clock is 'past the hour' (for example quarter past twelve) and 'i' when the clock is 'to the hour' (for example five to one). Do you remember all the numders from the previous lesson?

Chwarter wedi wyth

Now let's see how we can incorporate some more interesting sencetes together.

Am hanner dydd

When it comes to telling time, there are some mutations in how we say the numbers in Welsh. For example, the below sentences are something to keep in mind when telling the time. This mutation happens due to the feminite form of the nouns.

Ugain munud wedi bump

Twenty minutes past five

Dwy awr

Two hours

How to say past the hour like 15 past or to.

Am faint o'r gloch dych chi eisiau brecwast?

At what time do you want breakwast?

Hanner awr wedi naw

Half past nine

Okay, we are done for now! Hope you have learned some valuable things in this lesson. We have learned about some mutations of words in the feminine form and also some useful phrases that may come in handy when in Wales.

This lesson will be all about the animals and nature. Let's begin with some basics and then we will dive into some more interesting facts about the Welsh grammar.

Notice how the sentence below literally translates to 'has the farmer ten duck'. We say 'ten duck' even though there are many ducks. Important to remember that we always use singular nouns after numbers in Welsh.

You may wonder about the word 'mae'. It stands for 'is', 'there is' and 'are'. In Welsh, the verb, a word that describes action always comes before an adjective. So if we were to literally translate the sentences below is would be something like: 'is having the farmer ten ducks' and 'is hearing Jack the bird'.

Mae gan y ffermwr ddeg hwyaden

The farmer has ten ducks

Mae Jac yn clywed yr aderyn

Jack hears the bird

The plural for whales literally translates to 'some whales', and this is the concept we should apply across all plurals in Welsh.
